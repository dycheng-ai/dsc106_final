What Actually Causes U.S. Wildfires?

Folk belief blames lightning. Federal agencies say 85% are human-caused. But the climate data tells a deeper story—one about vapor, heat, and the invisible stress that turns a landscape into a tinderbox.

By Dylan Cheng, Vipra Bindal & Anwesha Nayak  ·  DSC 106 Project 3

1 / Fire season

Watch the 2024 fire season unfold

Each circle is a fire cell—a county-sized patch of land (about 50 km on a side) where GOES-16 detected active fire that week. Bigger circles mean more fire radiative power (MW). Drag the slider to walk through June to October.

Three burn corridors dominate: the Western mountains, a southern Plains band through Texas and Oklahoma, and the Mississippi Valley. Notice how the worst weeks cluster in midsummer—not a random pattern.

2 / Climate context

As dryness spikes, fires follow

The map background now shows each state's Vapor Pressure Deficit (VPD)—the gap between how much moisture the air could hold and how much it actually holds. High VPD means the atmosphere is aggressively pulling moisture out of vegetation, turning fuel bone-dry.

Move the slider week by week. The orange fire circles cluster almost exactly where the red-hot VPD zones light up—particularly across the West in July and August.

3 / State patterns

High-VPD states dominated the fire season

Zoom out. Each state is now colored on two axes: VPD → (left = low, right = high) and fire ↑ (bottom = low, top = high). States in the top-right corner had both high summer VPD and the most fire—California, Arizona, Nevada, Idaho.

Compare this to our earlier lightning map: Florida had the most lightning of any state and barely burned. Here, the pattern is far more consistent. Click any state to see its climate profile.

4 / The scatter

VPD is a strong predictor. Lightning is not.

One dot per state. The left panel plots mean summer VPD against total fire power—a clear upward trend (r = +0.64). The right panel shows the same fire data against prior-day lightning strikes—no meaningful trend (r = +0.04).

The West (orange) clusters at high VPD and high fire. The East (purple) sits at low VPD with little fire. Meanwhile, high-lightning states like Florida and Alabama appear scattered randomly against fire intensity.

5 / Humidity mirror

Low humidity tells the same story

Vapor pressure deficit and relative humidity are two sides of the same coin. This map swaps axes: states are now colored by minimum relative humidity—the lower, the more flammable.

The West lights up deep red for low humidity and high fire, while humid Eastern states stay cool and unburned. Whichever climate lens you use, the geography of fire risk is consistent—and it follows moisture, not lightning.

6 / The verdict

Climate variables outpredict lightning every time

Every GridMET variable ranked by how well it correlates with state-level wildfire intensity. VPD and low humidity sit at the top. Burning Index and Energy Release Component—derived indices that blend temperature, humidity, and wind—are similarly strong.

Lightning? Near the bottom of the list, barely distinguishable from noise. The atmosphere's dryness is what loads the gun. Ignition source—human or otherwise—is just the trigger.

Project Notes

Writeup

By Dylan Cheng, Vipra Bindal & Anwesha Nayak  ·  DSC 106 Project 3

Motivation

Our prior project established that lightning does not cause most U.S. wildfires—roughly 79% of fire cells in 2024 had no detectable lightning in the preceding 24 hours, matching federal agency estimates. But proving a negative leaves a question open: if not lightning, then what?

This project pivots to answering that question using the GridMET dataset (Abatzoglou 2013), a high-resolution (∼4 km) daily climate reanalysis covering the contiguous U.S. We focus on seven variables most linked to fire risk in the peer-reviewed literature: vapor pressure deficit (VPD), maximum temperature, minimum relative humidity, precipitation, wind speed, Burning Index (BI), and Energy Release Component (ERC). Our proposition is that climate-driven atmospheric dryness—especially VPD—is a far more consistent predictor of wildfire intensity than lightning strike frequency.

Design Rationale

We retained the same NYT-inspired visual style and scrollytelling architecture from Project 3 to maintain narrative continuity. The six stages follow the same "zoom in, zoom out" logic: an animated weekly map establishes the fire season spatially, then we introduce climate variables at progressively coarser scales (cell → state → ranked correlation).

Stage 2's VPD choropleth intentionally mirrors Stage 1's fire circle map so viewers can visually compare the two using the same slider, in the same geographic frame. The bivariate choropleth in Stage 3 uses an orange-red palette anchored at the high-VPD, high-fire corner rather than the high-lightning corner from Project 3; this immediately signals that the positive relationship is being visualized, not a surprising absence. The state-level scatter in Stage 4 uses a log scale on fire power to handle California's extreme values and plots both the VPD and lightning panels side by side so the contrast in predictive power is unmistakable without requiring any verbal argument.

Stage 5 repeats the bivariate idea for relative humidity (the inverse of VPD) to show that the finding is robust across moisture lenses, not an artifact of how VPD is calculated. Stage 6's horizontal bar chart provides a clean ranked summary of all correlations, explicitly showing lightning at the bottom—the natural ending to a story that began by debunking it.

Development Process

GridMET NetCDF files (seven variables, ∼760 MB total) were downloaded from northwestknowledge.net/metdata/data/, subset to the fire season (May–October 2024) and CONUS bounding box (lat 24–50, lon −125 to −65), coarsened from ∼4 km to 0.5° cells using block-mean aggregation, then joined on (week, lat_bin, lon_bin) to the GOES-16 fire cells from Project 3. State assignments used point-in-polygon via Shapely with the same topojson used by D3. The pipeline is fully reproducible via fetch_gridmet.py and takes approximately 15 minutes on a laptop.

State-level correlations (Pearson r on log-transformed fire power) were computed in Python across all 48 CONUS states. VPD achieved r = +0.64, minimum humidity r = −0.65, Burning Index r = +0.61, Energy Release Component r = +0.60, and prior-day lightning r = +0.04—confirming that climate moisture variables substantially outperform lightning as predictors of fire intensity.

Dylan led the GridMET data pipeline, correlation analysis, and Stage 4 scatter visualization. Vipra led the D3 animation and bivariate choropleth work for Stages 2–3 and 5. Anwesha led the narrative design, Stage 6 bar chart, and writeup. Total development: approximately 18–22 person-hours.
